Course Overview

Overview, Objectives, and Expectations

This course is for undergraduate and graduate students conducting or interested in learning how to conduct applied empirical research.

We will focus on the “research loop” and the basic steps in the social science research process: developing research questions, determining research design, collecting data, and analyzing data. The course will be run as both a seminar discussion on Mondays and a Lab/Workshop on Wednesdays, involving hands-on practice of research skills, including ethnographic fieldwork, qualitative analysis, research design, experimentation, content coding and analysis, and statistical analysis. Students will design a research project in the course. PhD students will typically, but not necessarily, work with their advisor on these projects.

The course is organized into six units, moving from foundations of research through measurement and validity, experimental design, non-experimental designs, qualitative and mixed methods, and finally analysis, interpretation, and communication. Statistical topics are not siloed into a separate unit—they are interleaved throughout the Wednesday labs and tied to whatever design topic is live that week. This course does not cover professional methods, such as contextual design or usability testing, that are already taught in other courses offered by the HCII. In addition, this course will not offer advanced statistical and computational analyses. The statistical topics covered are applicable to the writing and reading of empirical research papers: understanding data types, organizing data for statistical analysis, assessing data properties and correcting for anomalies, selecting appropriate analytical techniques, applying techniques most often used in empirical work such as ANOVA and regression, using the RStudio statistical package, and learning how to describe statistical tests for publication.

Each week we will discuss an empirical research topic. During the seminar sessions, we will discuss the method and approach assigned to that week. To prepare for that discussion, you will complete practice questions in Canvas and read assigned readings. During the lab sessions, you will complete, individually or as part of a group, hands-on activities related to that week’s topic. Please bring a laptop to every class.

Topics

Course Units

Foundations of Research

  • Scientific Reasoning and Research Approaches
  • Research Questions, Hypotheses, and R Orientation

Measurement and Validity

  • Measuring Variables and Research Validity
  • Ethics, Open Science, and Replication
  • Data Types and Descriptive Stats in R

Experimental Design

  • Experimental Design: Control and Between/Within Designs
  • Conducting a Research Study
  • Factorial and Mixed Designs
  • Designing an Experiment + Peer Critique
  • Quasi-Experimental and Single-Case Designs
  • t-tests and One-Way ANOVA in R
  • Confounding and Validity Threats
  • Factorial ANOVA in R

Non-Experimental Designs

  • Correlational Research
  • Correlation and Regression in R
  • Survey Research and Measurement
  • Designing a Survey + Likert Data in R
  • Observational and Archival Methods
  • Designing an Observation Protocol

Qualitative and Mixed Methods

  • Qualitative Research I: Foundations and Approaches
  • Thematic Coding + Intercoder Reliability (Cohen's κ)
  • Qualitative Research II: Interviews, Think-Aloud, Grounded Theory
  • Mixed Methods Design Workshop

Analysis, Interpretation, and Communication

  • Regression, Prediction, and Interpreting Results
  • Multiple Regression in R
